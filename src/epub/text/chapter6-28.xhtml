Tzŭ-kung said, "Suppose the case of a man extensively conferring benefits on the people, and able to assist all, what would you say of him? Might he be called perfectly virtuous?"440 The Master said, "Why speak only of virtue in connexion with him? Must he not have the qualities of a sage?441 Even Yao and Shun were still solicitous about this.442

"Now the man of perfect virtue, wishing to be established himself, seeks also to establish others; wishing to be enlarged himself, he seeks also to enlarge others.443

"To be able to judge of others by what is nigh in ourselves;—this may be called the art of virtue."444
