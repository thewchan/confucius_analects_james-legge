Section I
Books Included Under the Name of the Chinese Classics.

The books now recognised as of highest authority in China are comprehended under the denominations of "The five Ching1370" and "The four Shu1371." The term Ching is of textile origin, and signifies the warp threads of a web, and their adjustment. An easy application of it is to denote what is regular and insures regularity. As used with reference to books, it indicates their authority on the subjects of which they treat. "The five Ching" are the five canonical works, containing the truth upon the highest subjects from the sages of China, and which should be received as law by all generations. The term Shu simply mean writings or books, = the Pencil Speaking; it may be used of a single character, or of books containing thousands of characters.

"The five Ching" are: The Yi,1372 or, as it has been styled, The Book of Changes; the Shu,1373 or The Book of History; the Shih,1374 or The Book of Poetry; the Li Chi,1375 or Record of Rites; and the Chʽun Chʽiu,1376 or Spring and Autumn, a chronicle of events, extending from 733 to 481 BC. The authorship, or compilation rather, of all these works is loosely attributed to Confucius. But much of the Li Chi is form later hands. Of the Yi, the Shu, and the Shih, it is only the first that we find additions attributed to the philosopher himself, in the shape of appendixes. The Chʽun Chʽiu is the only one of the five Ching which can, with an approximation to correctness, be described as of his own "making."

"The Four Books" is an abbreviation for "The Books of the Four Philosophers."1377 The first is the Lun Yü,1378 or Digested Conversations, being occupied chiefly with the sayings of Confucius. He is the philosopher to whom it belongs. It appears in this work under the title of Confucian Analects. The second is the Ta Hsiao,1379 or Great Learning, now commonly attributed to Tsêng Shên,1380 a disciple of the sage. He is the philosopher of it. The third is the Chung Yung,1381 Doctrine of the Mean, as the name has often been translated, though it would be better to render it, as in the present edition, by The State of Equilibrium and Harmony. Its composition is ascribed to Kʽung Chi,1382 the grandson of Confucius. He is the philosopher of it. The fourth contains the works of Mencius.

This arrangement of the classical books, which is commonly supposed to have originated with the scholars of the Sung dynasty, is defective. The Great Learning and the Doctrine of the Mean are both found in the Record of Rites, being the thirty-ninth and twenty-eighth books respectively of that compilation, according to the best arrangement of it.

The oldest enumerations of the classical books specify only the five Ching. The Yüeh Chi,or Record of Music,1383 was sometimes added to those, making them the six Ching. A division was also made into nine Ching, consisting of the Yi, the Shih, the Shu, and the Chou Li,1384 or Ritual of Chou, the Yi Li,1385, or certain Ceremonial Usages, the Li Chi, and the three annotated editions of the Chʽun Chʽiu,1386 by Tso Chʽiu-ming,1387, Kung-yang Kao,1388, and Ku-liang Chʽih.1389 In the famous compilation of hte classical books, undertaken by order of Tʽai Tsung, the second emperor of the Tʽang dynasty (AD 627–649), and which appeared in the reign of his successor, there are thirteen Ching, viz. the Yi, the Shih, the Shu, the three editions of the Chʽun Chʽiu, the Li Chi, the Chou Li, the Yi Li, the Confucian Analects, the Ërh Ya,1390 a sort of ancient dictionary, the Hsiao Ching,1391 or Classic of Filial Piety, and the works of Mencius.

A distinction, however, was made among the works thus comprehended under the same common name; and Mencius, the Lun Yü, the Ta Hsiao, the Chung Yung, and the Hsiao Ching were spoken as the Hsiao Ching, or "smaller classics." It thus appears, contrary to the ordinary opinion on the subject, that the Ta Hsiao and the Chung Yung had been published as separate treatises before the Sung dynasty, and that Four Books, as distinguished from the greater Ching, had also previously found a place in the literature of China.1392
